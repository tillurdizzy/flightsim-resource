1. Clearance Delivery

Clearance Delivery is an air traffic controller whose function is to coordinate with other ATC facilities and provide departing aircraft with an instrument flight clearance. The pilot of a departing aircraft calls clearance delivery, states the aircraft identification and destination, and requests an IFR clearance.

The clearance delivery controller issues a clearance to the aircraft, providing the following elements:

  • Departure procedure -tells the pilot on what heading and at what altitude to depart from the airport.
  • Route of flight - the route that the pilot is to follow in order to arrive at the destination.
  • Altitude data - the initial altitude to be flown and the altitude to expect at a specified time, typically 10 minutes, after departure.
  • Departure control frequency - the first frequency on which to contact the departure controller when the tower instructs a frequency change.
  • Transponder code - the code to be entered in the transponder to assist radar equipped air traffic controllers in following the aircraft.
Big Jet 345, Gate B7, Boeing 737 with information Q, QNH1006, request clearance.
Big Jet 345, Metro Delivery, Cleared to Smallville, T1A departure, Squawk 3456

Pilot communication can be adversely affected by simultaneous or continuous transmissions. There are times when the controller is not aware of a blocked transmission, but a pilot is. On hearing a simultaneous transmission it can be helpful if a pilot informs ATC that the transmission was BLOCKED.

2. Ground - Push back

Ground Control is a controller in the tower cab whose responsibility it is to instruct all aircraft on the movement area of the airport. After clearance delivery has issued the clearance to the flight, its corresponding flight progress strip is passed to the ground controller. Thus, when a pilot radios the ground controller, the ground controller already knows the aircraft's route of flight and can assign an appropriate runway for that route, depending on which runways are available and the wind and weather conditions then prevailing.

Metro Ground, Big Jet 345, Gate B7, ready for push back.
Big Jet 345, cleared for push back, contact when ready for taxi.

3. Ground - Taxi to runway

Metro Ground, Big Jet 345, ready for taxi.
Big Jet 345, Metro Ground, taxi to holding point A1, hold short of Runway 18, contact Metro Tower 119.20

Communication is crucial to the safety of the flight during taxiing. Any mistake that causes the aircraft to enter a runway in error could be catastrophic. All taxi clearances will contain a clearance limit, which is the point at which the aircraft must stop unless further permission to proceed is given. Complex or lengthy taxi clearances should be noted down by crews. Any instructions to HOLD, HOLD POSITION or HOLD SHORT OF, shall be read back in full using the appropriate phrase – HOLDING or HOLD SHORT OF.

4. Tower - Taxi on to runway and take off clearance

Local Control has the responsibility for instructing all airborne aircraft within the vicinity of the airport, including instructions for aircraft to takeoff and land. The local controller may have to coordinate with the departure and arrival controllers in sequencing arriving and departing aircraft, and with the ground controller to authorize aircraft to taxi on or across active runways.

Metro Tower, Big Jet 345, approaching holding point C1.
Big Jet 345, Metro Tower, line up runway 27.
xxMetro Tower, Big Jet 345, approaching holding point C1.
Lining up runway 27, Big Jet 345.
Big Jet 345, runway 27, cleared for take-off.
Cleared for take-off, Big Jet 345.
Big Jet 345, contact Metro Radar 124.60
Contact Metro Radar on 124.60, Big Jet 345

VHF frequencies: Use five digits except where the final two digits of the frequency are both zero, in which case only the first three digits need to be transmitted.

5. Departure - Climb clearance and route changes

Metro Radar, Big Jet 345, passing 2300 feet climbing to 6000 feet.
Big Jet 345, fly heading 260 degrees, climb to FL100, no speed restrictions.
Fly heading 260 degrees, climb to FL100, no speed restrictions, Big Jet 345
Big Jet 345, fly direct BONNY, climb to FL360
Direct BONNY, climb to FL360, Big Jet 345
Big Jet 345, contact Northern Control, 132.60

Avoid heading and flight level confusion by appending the word ‘degrees’ to headings. Flight levels below FL100 are referred to as two digit numbers (e.g. 'Climb flight level eight zero'). Special care should be taken when enunciating ‘zero zero’.

9. Common Phrases You Should Know

Acknowledge

Let me know that you have understood this message.

Advise intentions

What are you planning to do?

Affirmative

Yes

Confirm

Is that correct?

Correction

Incorrect information, correct information follows.

Go ahead

State your request. (Does not mean to proceed!)

Hold

Stop at your current position.

Hold short of...

Proceed to and hold position at a specified point.

Negative

Incorrect, request denied, or No.

Proceed

Authorized to begin or continue moving.

Read back

Repeat the last message back.

Roger

Received and understood last transmission.

Say again

Repeat last transmission.

Standby

Wait, I will get back to you.

Unable

Can not comply with previous instructions.

Verify

Request confirmation of information.

Wilco

Received, understood and will comply.

To and For

Use of the words 'TO' and 'FOR' directly before a climb/descent instruction or change of heading can be confused as 'TWO' or 'FOUR'. Such confusion is avoided by using the mandatory words Flight Level or Heading immediately before the numbers.
